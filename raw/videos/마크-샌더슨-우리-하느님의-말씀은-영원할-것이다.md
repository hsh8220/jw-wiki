# 마크 샌더슨: “우리 하느님의 말씀은 영원할 것이다”

As Jehovah’s Witnesses, we truly love the Bible.
Why?
Because we know it is God’s Word.
That’s why we love reading it, we love studying it, and we love learning about its history.
When we pause for a moment and reflect on the many efforts that have been made through the centuries to hinder and even stop the distribution of the Bible, it becomes obvious that its Author, Jehovah, has been personally involved in preserving and making known his precious Word.
This is very much in harmony with the thought in Isaiah 40:8: “The green grass dries up, “the blossom withers, but the word of our God endures forever.” With that in mind, I’m happy to speak with you this month on a theme that’s very close to my heart: “The Word of Our God Endures Forever.” The more we come to appreciate the value of God’s Word, the more we’ll be moved to read it and, even more important, to apply it in our lives.
Jehovah gave us an outstanding gift when he inspired faithful men to record his precious Word.
It’s through the pages of God’s Word that we learn just how much God loves us.
Thus, his Word contains guidance on how to live.
It outlines his purpose in creating us.
It gives us his precious promises for life everlasting in peace and happiness.
No wonder the apostle Paul was inspired to write at 2 Timothy 3:16, 17: “All Scripture is inspired of God “and beneficial for teaching, for reproving, “for setting things straight, for disciplining in righteousness, “so that the man of God may be fully competent, completely equipped for every good work.” Of course, Satan the Devil does not want people to have access to these precious truths from our heavenly Father, Jehovah.
It’s no surprise that there have been many obstacles to making the Bible available.
While Satan may not have been directly behind all of these, he certainly has made use of them.
What are some of these obstacles, and how have they been overcome?
Let’s consider the first obstacle: language barriers.
The first 39 books of the Bible, which are often called the Old Testament, were written primarily in the Hebrew language.
These books were known by Hebrew-speaking servants of Jehovah.
However, as time went on, particularly after the destruction of Babylon, many Jews established communities in other nations of the earth.
Many of these Jews spoke Greek.
They no longer used Hebrew.
That meant that many Jews no longer had access to the Scriptures in a language they could understand.
Consider what had happened.
As Alexander the Great conquered more and more of the then-known world, Greek gradually became the common language of commerce and government throughout his empire.
If the Bible was to be understood by the Jews and by Jewish proselytes who were scattered throughout this vast Greek-speaking territory, it needed to be translated into Greek.
Beginning in about the year 250 B.C.E., the Hebrew Scriptures began to be translated into Greek.
In time, that Greek translation came to be known as the Septuagint.
It was widely and frequently used by the Christians in the first century who spoke Greek.
In fact, the writers of the Christian Greek Scriptures, which is often called the New Testament, frequently quoted directly from that translation.
So that obstacle had been overcome.
But there were many more language obstacles ahead.
As the Roman Empire began to flourish, Latin replaced Greek as a common language.
Now it was necessary to translate the Bible into Latin.
Some Latin translations of the Bible books began to appear.
But it was in the year 405 C.E.
that Jerome completed the definitive Latin translation of the Bible.
It came to be known as the Latin Vulgate.
Jerome’s translation would become the standard Bible used by Christendom for the next nearly 1,000 years.
However, Latin soon became the language of the learned and the elite, so the common people could no longer understand the Bible’s message.
During that long period, there were sincere men that translated portions of the Bible into some additional languages, but these translations were, of course, all made by hand.
Such handwritten copies of the Bible were rare and expensive.
Therefore, access to the Bible in vernacular, or common languages, was very limited.
But language was not the only obstacle that threatened to prevent the distribution of God’s Word.
Consider a second factor: direct opposition to Bible translation and distribution from religious leaders.
That opposition started to increase in 1229 when the local church Council of Toulouse decreed: “We prohibit also that the laity should be permitted to have the books of the Old or the New Testament.” In 1234, the Council of Tarragona decreed: “No one should possess the books of the Old or the New Testament “in the vernacular [language], “and if anyone possesses them, “let him deliver them, within eight days “from the promulgation of this decree, to the local bishop, so that they may be burned.” Thus, during a very long period in history, there was limited access to the Bible in the languages spoken by common people.
But did that mean that the Word of God would not continue to endure?
Neither language barriers nor opposition from religious leaders could stop God’s Word from being made known.
Even in those dark times, there were individuals who loved God’s Word and who worked hard to translate it and to make it available to others.
Consider John Wycliffe and his followers who were called the Lollards.
John Wycliffe and his associates completed the translation of the Bible into English about the year 1382.
They eagerly hand copied the translation and then traveled from village to village reading the Bible to the common people.
Despite tremendous opposition from the church, Wycliffe and his followers made the Bible available to many honesthearted people in the English language.
After Wycliffe died in 1384, great opposition came to his followers.
Many had land and property confiscated.
Others were burned at the stake.
In response to the work of Wycliffe and the Lollards, in 1408, Thomas Arundel, Archbishop of Canterbury, declared: “We therefore decree and ordain “that no man shall, hereafter, by his own authority, “translate any text of the Scripture “into English, or any other tongue, .
.
.
upon pain of greater excommunication.” Did such opposition wipe out efforts to translate and make the Bible available?
Absolutely not!
The work of Wycliffe and his associates stimulated interest in the Bible throughout England and even as far as central Europe.
Religious leaders were not able to silence the Word of God.
In fact, within just a few decades after Wycliffe’s death, two factors led to a tremendous increase in the availability of the Bible.
The first, about the year 1454, was the development of printing with movable type.
This invention would in time allow for the printing of large quantities of Bibles in many languages.
The second factor was the compiling and printing of the Hebrew and Greek original language text of the Holy Scriptures.
This allowed many translators to have access to the original language texts so that they then could produce high-quality translations in their own languages.
What was the result?
In the 1500’s across Europe, fresh translations were made and printed in vernacular, or common languages, including German, English, Italian, French, Dutch, Czech, Danish, Swedish, Spanish, Slovenian, and many others.
However, opposition continued.
Bible translators were frequently hounded by the Inquisition, and some translators like William Tyndale and John Rogers were even put to death for their work.
Nonetheless, the Word of God continued to endure and be made available to more and more language groups.
By the early 1800’s, Bible translation began to speed up.
Bible societies and intrepid translators began to work in many parts of the earth so that the Bible began to appear in languages of Asia, Africa, the Pacific islands, as well as in indigenous languages of the Americas.
From 1800 to 1899, some portions of the Bible were translated into some 400 languages, and from 1900 to 1950, more than 500 languages were added.
Consider what has been accomplished with the New World Translation.
As of this month, the New World Translation has been produced in whole or in part in 149 languages, with an additional 37 languages approved.
It’s obvious that neither language barriers nor opposition from religious leaders was able to stop the distribution of God’s Word.
Jehovah has protected it through the ages.
It’s just as Psalm 12:6, 7 says: “The sayings of Jehovah are pure; “they are like silver refined in an earthen furnace, “purified seven times.
“You will guard them, O Jehovah; “you will protect each one of them from this generation forever.” Yes, Jehovah has protected each one of his precious sayings down through the ages.
But consider a third obstacle.
Although the Bible is now available in whole or in part in almost 3,000 languages, many people are no longer interested in reading it.
In our modern age, people have become increasingly skeptical and irreligious.
Scientists have extolled the theory of evolution and have made it appear intelligent to doubt the existence of God.
At the same time, secular philosophy has encouraged people to make life decisions according to what feels right to them.
What is the result?
A world where people are often guided by their own selfish, fleshly desires.
Instead of leading to happiness, many people are left feeling empty and dissatisfied.
How is this third obstacle overcome?
Around the world, people are opening God’s Word, the Bible, and reading it for themselves.
They’re recognizing that its thoughts are not simply human wisdom.
As they apply its principles in their lives, they find contentment and joy.
They develop self-respect and dignity from living according to the upright moral standards outlined by our Creator.
As Psalm 119:97-100 states: “How I do love your law!
“I ponder over it all day long.

“I have more insight than all my teachers, “because I ponder over your reminders.
“I act with more understanding than older men, because I observe your orders.” What a powerful proof that the Word of God endures forever.
While the advice of human philosophers is frequently flawed, the Bible’s counsel works.
If we apply it, it can help us either to overcome or to cope with any problem or situation that we may face.
What is more, by explaining God’s purpose for the future, it gives us a solid hope this earth will be restored to the Paradise that it was when it was first created.
It will be filled with peace and righteousness.
What a wonderful gift Jehovah gave us through the Bible!
Yes, languages barriers, opposition from religious leaders, and even an increasingly irreligious world cannot stop God’s Word.

Read it every day, study it, meditate on the deep thoughts that it contains, and then apply those priceless teachings in your life.
